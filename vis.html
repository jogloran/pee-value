Pee Value: A Bayesian Bladder Model

Predicting P(urge in next N minutes) from drink history using Sequential Monte Carlo

Given a log of what you've drunk and when you last felt the urge to urinate, can we predict when you'll need to go next? This page walks through the probabilistic model behind Bayesian Bladder Urge Estimation — a Sequential Monte Carlo system that learns your personal physiology from observations.

Based on pee_value.py  ·  Interactive explainer

1. How Fluid Moves Through You

When you drink something, it doesn't reach your bladder instantly — it's absorbed by your gut over roughly 20 minutes, then excreted over the next couple of hours. We model this with a difference-of-exponentials kernel:

k(τ) = exp(−τ / τexcrete) − exp(−τ / τabsorb),   τ > 0

Here τ is the time elapsed since you drank. The first term (slow decay) models excretion; the second (fast decay) models absorption. Their difference gives a curve that rises as fluid is absorbed and falls as it's excreted. Think of it as the "bladder loading" impulse response for a single drink.

2. Bladder Load — Superposition

The total bladder load at time t is just the sum of all drink contributions:

B(t) = Σi Vi · αi · k(t − ti)

Each drink contributes its own scaled kernel. The scaling factor αi captures the diuretic effect of different beverages: coffee contains caffeine, a diuretic, so it loads the bladder more than its volume alone would suggest; tea less so.

3. The Hazard Rate — A Sigmoid Gate

Bladder load alone doesn't tell us about urge probability. We need to map it to a rate: how many urge events per minute are we expecting right now?

The instantaneous hazard rate is modelled as a sigmoid function of bladder load. Below some threshold θ the bladder is comfortable and urges are rare; above it they become increasingly likely. The steepness β controls how sharply the transition happens:

λ(t) = λ0 · σ( β · (B(t) − θ) )

where σ(x) = 1/(1 + e−x) is the logistic sigmoid and λ0 is the baseline urge rate (events per minute).

4. Survival Probability — Silence Is Evidence

The model uses a point-process framework. Given a hazard rate λ(t), the probability of no urge occurring in a window [t, t+h] is:

P(no urge in [t, t+h]) = exp( −∫tt+h λ(s) ds )

This is the survival function of the process. Its complement gives the prediction:

P(urge in next h min) = 1 − exp( −∫tt+h λ(s) ds )

The integral is approximated numerically using the midpoint rule with 50 steps. A crucial insight: even the absence of observed urges during a quiet interval is informative — it penalises particles (parameter hypotheses) that predicted high hazard during that interval.

5. The Particle Filter — Many Hypotheses at Once

We don't know the true values of λ0, β, θ, τabsorb, τexcrete for any individual. Instead of fitting a single set of parameters, we maintain N particles — each particle is one complete hypothesis about all parameters, carrying a weight that reflects how well it explains the observed data so far.

This is Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC), also called a particle filter. It works in three steps:

  1. Initialise — sample parameters from broad prior distributions (log-normal).
  2. Reweight — multiply each particle's weight by the likelihood of observed data under its parameters.
  3. Resample — when the effective sample size ESS = 1/Σwi² falls below 50% of N, duplicate high-weight particles and discard low-weight ones (systematic resampling).

7. Summary

The model chains four ideas together:

  1. A physiological kernel turns drink events into a continuous bladder-load signal.
  2. A sigmoid hazard maps that load to an instantaneous urge rate.
  3. A survival integral converts the hazard into a prediction horizon probability, and lets silence serve as evidence.
  4. A particle filter maintains a full posterior over individual physiology parameters, updating from both urge observations and quiet periods alike.

The result is a system that is simultaneously generative (it can simulate urge patterns) and discriminative (it learns your personal physiology from just a few urge observations), all without ever fitting a fixed set of parameters or requiring labelled training data beyond your own history.
