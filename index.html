Pee Value: A Bayesian Bladder Model

Predicting P(urge in next N minutes) from drink history using Sequential Monte Carlo

Given a log of what you've drunk and when you last felt the urge to urinate, can we predict when you'll need to go next? This page walks through the probabilistic model behind Bayesian Bladder Urge Estimation — a Sequential Monte Carlo system that learns your personal physiology from observations.

Based on pee_value.py  ·  Interactive explainer

1. How Fluid Moves Through You

When you drink something, it doesn't reach your bladder instantly — it's absorbed by your gut over roughly 20 minutes, then excreted over the next couple of hours. We model this with a difference-of-exponentials kernel:

$$\kappa(\tau) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{\tau}{\tau_{\text{excrete}}}\right) - \exp\!\left(-\frac{\tau}{\tau_{\text{absorb}}}\right), \qquad \tau > 0$$

Here $\tau$ is the time elapsed since you drank. The first term (slow decay) models excretion; the second (fast decay) models absorption. Their difference gives a curve that rises as fluid is absorbed and falls as it's excreted.

2. Bladder Load — Superposition

The total bladder load at time $t$ is just the sum of all drink contributions:

$$B(t) = \sum_i V_i \cdot \alpha_i \cdot \kappa(t - t_i)$$

Each drink $i$, consumed at time $t_i$ in volume $V_i$ ml, contributes its own scaled kernel. The scaling factor $\alpha_i$ captures the diuretic effect of different beverages: coffee contains caffeine, a diuretic, so it loads the bladder more than its volume alone would suggest; tea less so. These multipliers are fixed presets, not learned from data. The kernel shape — governed by $\tau_{\text{absorb}}$ and $\tau_{\text{excrete}}$ — is learned per person, along with the hazard parameters $\lambda_0, \beta, \theta$ introduced in the next section.

3. The Hazard Rate — A Sigmoid Gate

Bladder load alone doesn't tell us about urge probability. We need to map it to a rate: how many urge events per minute are we expecting right now?

The instantaneous hazard rate is modelled as a sigmoid function of bladder load. Below some threshold $\theta$ the bladder is comfortable and urges are rare; above it they become increasingly likely. The steepness $\beta$ controls how sharply the transition happens:

$$\lambda(t) = \lambda_0 \cdot \sigma\!\bigl(\beta\,(B(t) - \theta)\bigr)$$

where $\sigma(x) = 1/(1+e^{-x})$ is the logistic sigmoid and $\lambda_0$ is the maximum urge rate — the ceiling approached when the bladder is very full (events per minute).

θ shifts the sigmoid left or right — it's the bladder load at which urges become likely, so a higher θ means a larger-capacity bladder. β controls how abruptly urgency kicks in: low β gives a gradual ramp, high β a sharp threshold. λ₀ is the overall rate ceiling — even a full bladder can only produce urges as fast as λ₀ allows, so it sets how frequently urge events occur at worst.

4. Survival Probability — Silence Is Evidence

The model uses a point-process framework. Given a hazard rate $\lambda(t)$, the probability of no urge occurring in a window $[t,\,t+h]$ is:

$$P(\text{no urge in } [t,\,t{+}h]) = \exp\!\left(-\int_t^{t+h} \lambda(s)\,ds\right)$$

This is the survival function of the process. Its complement gives the prediction:

$$P(\text{urge in next } h \text{ min}) = 1 - \exp\!\left(-\int_t^{t+h} \lambda(s)\,ds\right)$$
How is the integral computed numerically?

Because $\lambda(s)$ has no closed-form antiderivative, the integral is approximated numerically. The interval $[t, t+h]$ is divided into $n$ equal steps of width $\Delta s = h/n$, and $\lambda$ is evaluated at the midpoint of each step:

$$\int_t^{t+h} \lambda(s)\,ds \;\approx\; \Delta s \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \lambda\!\left(t + \left(k + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\Delta s\right)$$

This is the midpoint rule; the implementation uses $n = 50$ steps, which is accurate enough given that $\lambda$ varies smoothly on the timescale of minutes.

5. The Particle Filter — Many Hypotheses at Once

We can't know your physiology in advance. Instead, the model keeps 500 particles alive simultaneously — each one a different guess at your five parameters. Below, each dot is one such guess. Its position encodes two of them ($\theta$ on the x-axis, $\lambda_0$ on the y-axis); its size shows how strongly we currently believe it.

Sampling the prior

Each particle starts as a random draw from a prior distribution — our best guess at plausible human physiology before seeing any data. All five parameters are positive and span orders of magnitude across people, so they are drawn from log-normal distributions: a value whose logarithm is normally distributed. This keeps samples strictly positive and lets the tails reach both very sensitive and very tolerant bladders.

Weight update equations

Silence over $[t_a, t_b]$: in a Poisson process, the probability of zero arrivals is the $k=0$ term $e^{-\mu}\mu^0/0! = e^{-\mu}$, where $\mu = \int_{t_a}^{t_b}\!\lambda_i(s)\,ds$ is the expected count. So:

$$w_i \;\leftarrow\; w_i \cdot \exp\!\left(-\int_{t_a}^{t_b} \lambda_i(s)\,ds\right)$$

Urge at $t^*$: the probability of an event in an infinitesimal window $[t^*, t^*\!+dt]$ is $\lambda_i(t^*)\,dt$, giving:

$$w_i \;\leftarrow\; w_i \cdot \lambda_i(t^*)$$

Void: a voluntary act carries no information about the latent parameters, so weights are unchanged. The drink history is cleared, resetting $B(t)$ to zero and returning $\lambda(t)$ toward $\lambda_0 \cdot \sigma(-\beta\theta) \approx 0$.

Despite the Bayesian framing, the implementation is remarkably mundane:

t = 0 history = [] # drink log: [(time, volume, type), ...] particles = sample $N$ hypotheses from prior # hazard rate for a particle at time $s$: def $\lambda$(s, particle, history): $B = \sum_i$ history$_i$.volume $\cdot$ $\alpha$(history$_i$.type) $\cdot$ $\kappa(s - t_i,\; \tau_\text{absorb},\; \tau_\text{excrete})$ return $\lambda_0 \cdot \sigma\!\bigl(\beta\,(B - \theta)\bigr)$ for each event in chronological order: # account for silence since the last event for each particle: $\mu = \int_t^{t_\text{event}} \lambda(s)\,ds$ # midpoint rule particle.weight *= $\exp(-\mu)$ normalise weights t = event.time if event is a drink: history.append(event) # future $\lambda$ calls will include this drink elif event is an urge: for each particle: particle.weight *= $\lambda(t)$ normalise weights if $\text{ESS} < N/2$: resample elif event is a void: history = [] # bladder emptied; weights unchanged

After processing events, each particle has accumulated a weight — a single number encoding how consistently that hypothesis predicted what actually happened. A particle that kept forecasting urgency during quiet periods, or predicted calm during an urge, has had its weight repeatedly cut. One that called it right each time has grown.

To answer a question — "will I need to pee in the next 30 minutes?" — you don't pick the winner. You ask every particle and take a weighted average:

p_urge = 0 for each particle: p_urge += particle.weight $\cdot\;\Bigl(1 - \exp\!\Bigl(-\int_t^{t+h} \lambda(s)\,ds\Bigr)\Bigr)$

Particles we trust more pull the answer toward their prediction; marginal particles barely contribute. The same weighted average gives estimates — and uncertainties — for any parameter: $\theta$, $\lambda_0$, $\beta$, and so on.

Over time, bad particles accumulate wrong predictions and their weights shrink toward zero. They still exist in the array — they just contribute nothing to the answer.

The effective sample size $\mathrm{ESS} = 1/\!\sum_i w_i^2$ measures how many particles are actually doing useful work. With 500 equal-weight particles, $\mathrm{ESS} = 500$. If one particle has grabbed nearly all the weight, $\mathrm{ESS} \approx 1$ — you effectively have a single hypothesis.

When $\mathrm{ESS}$ falls below $N/2$, the filter resamples: it discards the near-zero particles and duplicates the heavy ones, resetting all weights to $1/N$. The population stays healthy. Drag the slider below to see this in action, then hit Resample when it activates.

The resampling algorithm works like a weighted dartboard. Drag the slider to give particles unequal weights — each block is one particle, wide if heavy, a sliver if near zero. The white needles are evenly spaced; each picks the particle it lands on:

7. Summary

The model chains four ideas together:

  1. A physiological kernel turns drink events into a continuous bladder-load signal.
  2. A sigmoid hazard maps that load to an instantaneous urge rate.
  3. A survival integral converts the hazard into a prediction horizon probability, and lets silence serve as evidence.
  4. A particle filter maintains a full posterior over individual physiology parameters, updating from both urge observations and quiet periods alike.

The result is a system that is simultaneously generative (it can simulate urge patterns) and discriminative (it learns your personal physiology from just a few urge observations), all without ever fitting a fixed set of parameters or requiring labelled training data beyond your own history.
